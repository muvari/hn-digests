2 Space

First atmosphere found on Earth-like planet in habitable zone of distant star

450 points · 269 comments · bbc.com

Researchers led by Harvard's Dr. Collin Cherubim report the first detection of an atmosphere on a rocky planet sitting in the habitable zone of another star: LHS 1140 b, 48 light-years away, orbiting a small red star. The only gas confirmed so far is helium (likely in the upper atmosphere), which alone can't support life, but the authors believe heavier, more biologically relevant gases may exist lower down. The finding, published in Science, doesn't demonstrate life but is described as a major step toward answering whether Earth-like worlds with atmospheres exist beyond our solar system.

What the comments said

  • A long sub-thread debated realistic interstellar propulsion for reaching a world "only" 48 light-years away — laser-driven solar sails, antimatter, and Orion-style nuclear pulse drives were floated, alongside sober reminders that even our fastest current probes would take a million-plus years to arrive.
  • Several commenters pushed back on the claim that helium "can't support life," arguing it only rules out life as we know it (carbon-based, terrestrial biochemistry), while others countered that helium's chemical inertness makes it a poor building block for any biology, familiar or not.
  • There was interest in solar gravitational lens telescopes as a way to directly image such exoplanets, with pushback that such a mission would need to fly a probe roughly 500 AU out, opposite the target, and stay useful for decades — a huge engineering ask.
  • A few threads clarified the science for confused readers: the confirmed signal is helium escaping the upper atmosphere, taken as evidence of a retained atmosphere overall, while the composition of the lower atmosphere (possibly nitrogen, water vapor, CO2) remains inferred rather than directly measured.

7 Career & Essays

Three ways people respond to a problem (other than solving it)

236 points · 135 comments · improvesomething.today

Consultant Brian Kerr catalogs responses to organizational problems besides solving them: "pushing problems around" (local optimization that just relocates the pain elsewhere), "preserving problems" (the Shirky Principle — institutions that quietly depend on a problem's continued existence), and "promoting new problems" (solving one issue just surfaces the next one in priority). He argues consultants shouldn't blame individuals for pushing problems around — they're playing the incentives they're handed — and that the real skill is accepting you'll never "finish" solving problems, then choosing a few worth actually fixing.

What the comments said

  • Many readers proposed a "fourth" response the article missed — denial — with one describing their own current burnout-driven coping strategy of deliberately deferring, deflecting, or resting rather than always engaging.
  • Commenters extended "preserving problems" beyond institutions to individuals, describing how in-house "experts" can have a perverse incentive to keep the root cause of a problem unsolved because their expertise (and job security) depends on the problem persisting.
  • Several linked the framework to established models — the four risk-management strategies (avoid/mitigate/transfer/accept) and the five stages of grief — as parallel ways of categorizing problem responses.
  • A thread on "doing nothing" as a valid response split commenters: some argued indecision offloads risk from the decision-maker onto the whole team, while others said explicitly choosing to wait (versus passively failing to decide) is itself a legitimate, active decision.
8 Neuroscience

EEG shows brain can simultaneous encode two speech streams

250 points · 167 comments · journals.plos.org

A PLOS Biology study used EEG on normal-hearing adults switching attention between two competing speech streams every 15-30 seconds amid background noise, finding that neural tracking of the newly attended stream begins before the brain fully disengages from the previous one — a brief window where both streams are simultaneously encoded. This transition correlated with a drop in EEG alpha power (a marker of cognitive effort), and the researchers also found evidence that listeners "reset" their lexical/predictive context after switching attention, using LLM-built context-accumulation models to probe this.

What the comments said

  • Multiple commenters — a pilot/radio officer, a teacher, and others — shared firsthand experience of routinely tracking two audio/conversation streams simultaneously, generally unsurprised by the finding.
  • A popular sub-thread discussed the related phenomenon of reading aloud "on autopilot" while thinking about something else entirely, with several describing how this skill developed gradually as their brain offloaded the vocalization to a subroutine, freeing attention for other thought.
  • Commenters connected it to air-traffic control work, noting controllers routinely monitor multiple radio frequencies in each ear and have to filter overlapping calls without any formal training for it.
  • One thread noted a related "delayed audio feedback" effect — hearing your own voice with a slight delay (e.g., through a neighboring open-plan office speaker) disrupts fluent speech, illustrating how fragile single-stream processing can be.
9 Science & History

How Has Roman Concrete Lasted for Millennia? 1,900-Year-Old Latrine Offers Clues

258 points · 216 comments · smithsonianmag.com

Researchers studying an untouched, 1,900-year-old latrine at Hadrian's Villa near Rome found that beyond the well-known pozzolanic reaction (volcanic ash plus lime), a second process called carbonation — atmospheric CO2 reacting with calcium compounds to form self-healing calcite — plays a bigger role in Roman concrete's longevity than previously thought. Because "nobody restores a latrine," the sample offered a rare 19-century-long undisturbed experiment; the findings, published in Science Advances, build on 2023 work on quicklime-driven self-healing and could inform lower-carbon modern concrete design, since concrete production accounts for roughly 8% of global CO2 emissions.

What the comments said

  • The most common explanation offered for why modern concrete fails sooner: steel rebar corrodes and cracks the surrounding concrete, whereas Roman concrete had no rebar; commenters discussed stainless-steel and fiberglass rebar alternatives that dramatically extend lifespan but cost more.
  • Several argued modern concrete's shorter lifespan is largely a deliberate economic tradeoff — engineers optimize to meet minimum code requirements cheaply rather than for multi-century durability, since most infrastructure is built to a budget and expected to be replaced anyway.
  • A tangent on survivorship bias: commenters noted that plenty of 1930s-era concrete was actually low quality and has already been replaced, so what we see today (like WPA-stamped sidewalks) is a biased sample of the best work from that era.
  • One highly-cited comparison: two piers built side-by-side in the 1940s, one with carbon steel and one with stainless steel rebar, show dramatically different aging — offered as real-world evidence for the rebar-corrosion theory.

12 Programming Languages

Solod: Go can be a better C

206 points · 143 comments · solod.dev

Solod ("So") is a new strict-subset-of-Go language that compiles to readable C11, aimed at Go developers who want systems-level control without learning a new language, and at C programmers who want Go's tooling and structure. It has zero runtime by default (no GC, no hidden allocations — everything stack-allocated unless you opt into the heap via the standard library), native C interop without CGO, and works with existing Go tooling (LSP, `go test`, linting); as of v0.2 it supports networking, WebAssembly, and freestanding mode, with concurrency support planned for v0.3.

What the comments said

  • A detailed technical thread examined how Solod handles pointers to stack-allocated data (the classic "returning a pointer to a local" problem), concluding it behaves exactly like C — the FAQ itself admits it doesn't prevent dangling pointers or leaks, so commenters pushed back on the "safety of Go" framing as a bit of a stretch.
  • Several Go game developers weighed in that Go's garbage collector is a non-issue in practice for gameplay-focused (not performance-maxing) projects, tempering some of the project's core "GC is a problem" motivation.
  • One commenter wondered how Solod will implement generics given C output, guessing monomorphization (unlike Go's own root-type-based generic runtime trick) is the likely approach since C compilers are fast.
  • A tangential wish surfaced for a Go/Haskell-like language usable for mobile app development with over-the-air update capability, as an alternative to Flutter/React Native, given how slow app-store review can be relative to daily iteration.

16 Retrocomputing

The Zilog Z80 has turned 50

222 points · 76 comments · goliath32.com

A deep retrospective on the Zilog Z80, launched July 1976, tracing its lineage from the Datapoint 2200 terminal project through Intel's 8008, and detailing the 8008's register architecture, memory addressing, and interrupt handling as historical context for the Z80's design. The Z80 (and its 8080/8085-compatible siblings) became a de facto standard for 8-bit microcomputers, CP/M, and Microsoft BASIC, spawned clones like the Game Boy's Sharp LR35902, and survived in industrial/embedded use until Zilog finally discontinued the original part just two years ago — the author, too young to have used it in its home-computing heyday, built their own Z80 system as a teenager and interviewed old teachers with firsthand stories about it.

What the comments said

  • A wave of nostalgia from ZX-81, ZX Spectrum, and TI-84/TI-83 calculator owners, recalling how the Z80 was many people's first exposure to assembly and low-level CPU concepts, often learned by mapping BASIC concepts (GOTO, GOSUB, IF) onto Z80 instructions.
  • Commenters noted the Z80 lives on today inside TI graphing calculators (still sold and used by millions of students) via the eZ80 variant, and pointed to open-core/cycle-accurate clone chips as options for hobbyists now that original stock is drying up.
  • Two detailed personal anecdotes stood out: one about a father's failed attempt to add peripherals to a TRS-80 via its BUSREQ pin (mislabeled as a test pin, wired into the tri-state buffers), and a claim (disputed by other commenters) that the Game Boy Advance embeds an actual Z80 core for backward compatibility rather than the more likely Sharp SM83.
  • Older commenters shared decades-old memories of building Z80 kits with oscilloscopes and logic probes, and recommended classic references like Rodnay Zaks's "Programming the Z80."
