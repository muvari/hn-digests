1 Software

Your 'app' could have been a webpage (so I fixed it for you)

809 points · 479 comments · danq.me

The author was annoyed that a travel agency required installing a bloated Android app just to view an itinerary that was really just text, images, and PDF links. He reverse-engineered the app by rooting an Android emulator and intercepting its traffic with HTTP Toolkit, discovering it simply fetches JSON from an API endpoint keyed on the user's concatenated username and password. He then wrote a Ruby script to pull that JSON on a schedule and render it as a tiny, password-protected static webpage (about 0.05MB versus a 43–124MB app) that strips out the tracking and ads, and shared it with his tour group.

What the comments said

  • Widespread praise for the reverse-engineering write-up and the underlying principle, with many agreeing that most such "apps" are pointless wrappers around content that should just be a webpage.
  • Several asked why he didn't release a general-purpose tool for converting these apps; the author (who showed up in the thread) explained he's wary of legal/ToS risk from hitting a private API and may open-source it after the trip ends.
  • A recurring theory is that companies push apps specifically to enable tracking and push notifications rather than to serve users better.
  • Some nostalgia for the original promise of PWAs before app-store economics (and the 30% cut) pushed companies toward native apps instead.
  • A few pushed back that web apps are often just as bad, so the app-vs-web divide isn't as clean as the post suggests.
2 Energy

Japan develops a method to recover up to 90% of lithium from used EV batteries

728 points · 188 comments · tech.supercarblondie.com

The article reports that Japanese recycling engineers have developed a process that recovers around 90% of the lithium from used EV batteries, far above the under-50% typical of conventional methods, by substituting recovered lithium hydroxide for sodium hydroxide when processing battery "black mass." It claims the technique also cuts recycling-related carbon emissions by roughly 40% and frames the breakthrough as a way for Japan to reduce reliance on imported battery minerals, while noting only about 14% of Japan's used lithium-ion batteries currently enter formal recycling, with plans to scale up production by 2027 and 2035.

What the comments said

  • Many commenters criticized the article itself as poorly written and sensationalized clickbait recycling old (April) news, and asked for a link to the original NHK World source instead.
  • Several pointed out that 90% lithium recovery is already close to industry standard, with some processes hitting 95%+, undercutting the "breakthrough" framing.
  • A well-received technical explainer noted lithium recycling is inherently easier than mining because battery "black mass" is already a high-purity source, drawing a parallel to near-100% recycling rates for lead-acid batteries.
  • A tangent debated why Japan lags on EV adoption despite favorable conditions (kei cars, dense infrastructure, mandatory inspections), with some blaming industry pride or Toyota's complacency after its hybrid-era lead.
  • A side thread noted battery recycling's costs and environmental burdens are sometimes outsourced to countries with laxer regulation, echoing the history of lead-acid recycling.

4 AI & ML

Bonsai 27B: A 27B-Class model that runs on a phone

590 points · 210 comments · prismml.com

PrismML announces Bonsai 27B, a heavily quantized version of Qwen3.6 27B that they say is the first 27B-class model capable of running on a phone. It ships as a 5.9GB "Ternary" build (1.71 effective bits per weight) aimed at laptops, and a 3.9GB "1-bit" build (1.125 effective bits per weight) small enough to fit an iPhone 17 Pro's memory budget. Across a 15-benchmark suite the two variants retain 95% and 90% of the full-precision model's performance respectively — with math, coding, and tool-calling holding up especially well — and the models are multimodal, support 262K context, and are released under Apache 2.0.

What the comments said

  • Clarifying discussion of what "1-bit" actually means here, distinguishing Bonsai's true ternary (~1.71 bit) and true binary (~1.125 bit) variants from the looser "1.58-bit" convention popularized by earlier work.
  • Some skepticism from a commenter with hands-on quantization experience that real-world degradation, especially multi-turn tool-calling reliability, may be understated by the benchmark averages.
  • Multiple people reported the released weights don't yet run cleanly in LM Studio, Unsloth, or mainline llama.cpp, expecting fixes within days; one linked PrismML's own llama.cpp fork with Metal/CPU patches.
  • Initial confusion over whether this was a phone app rather than a model release, resolved with links to the Hugging Face model collection.
  • Speculative math in the thread estimated this quantization approach could scale to roughly a 100–110B parameter model still fitting a single 16GB GPU.

The Tower Keeps Rising

464 points · 222 comments · lucumr.pocoo.org

Armin Ronacher uses Bruegel's Tower of Babel as a metaphor for AI-assisted software development: coding agents remove the friction that once forced developers to synchronize their mental models of a shared codebase, since anyone can now ask an agent to make a local change without consulting anyone else. 7 Dev Tools

The git history command

436 points · 310 comments · lalitm.com

The author highlights an under-discussed experimental Git feature, git history, shipped across the 2.54 and 2.55 releases, with three subcommands: fixup (fold staged changes into an old commit and rebase all descendant branches), reword (edit an old commit message and rebase on top), and split (interactively split one commit into two). All three are atomic, refusing to run if they'd produce a conflict, which he argues delivers much of what draws people to the git alternative jj without requiring a full workflow switch — though it still lacks jj's first-class conflict handling, operation log, and working-copy-as-commit model.

What the comments said

  • Several pointed out this essentially formalizes three common rebase -i use cases as lower-friction commands, but only when there are no merge conflicts to resolve.
  • A large sub-thread debated whether interactive rebase is really as scary as the article claims, with some noting git reflog and backup branches make it fully recoverable, while others countered that even a fully understood conflict is still stressful to resolve.
  • Developers liked split for helping juniors break up oversized PRs, though some wished it could split an entire branch rather than a single commit.
  • A philosophical disagreement emerged over history rewriting itself: some prefer an "accountant" approach of never editing history and only appending fix commits, while others argued a commit graph doesn't have to represent literal chronological history.
  • A dissenting voice called rewriting shared ancestor commits "repo defiling," arguing it's cleaner to make the desired change upstream and merge it forward instead.

9 Energy Policy

Australian energy retailers must offer three hours of free daytime electricity

274 points · 375 comments · lenergy.com.au

From 1 July 2026, Australia's "Solar Sharer Offer" requires energy retailers in NSW, South Australia, and South-East Queensland to give households with a smart meter at least three hours of free electricity around midday, passing on cheap midday solar power that previously pushed wholesale prices negative without ever reaching consumer bills. Following public consultation, a 24 kWh/day usage cap was added to keep the scheme financially sustainable, a level most solar households are unlikely to hit. The government estimates households could save $100 to $1,100 a year depending on how much of their usage (appliances, EV charging, battery charging) they can shift into the free window.

What the comments said

  • Many commenters welcomed redistributing midday solar oversupply so non-solar households benefit too, reducing grid stress in a "herd immunity"-style framing, though some were surprised everyone gets the same fixed time slot instead of staggered windows.
  • Several noted the irony that market-based mechanisms are only embraced by the industry now that renewables became the cheap option, contrasting it with historical fossil-fuel lobbying against such intervention.
  • One thread flagged a perverse incentive: an oversized resistive-heating hot water tank timed to the free window is now cheaper than an efficient heat-pump tank, despite being worse environmentally.
  • Geographic scope generated clarifying back-and-forth: Western Australia is excluded (separate grid, expected 2027) and Victoria wasn't part of the initial July launch, though replies noted Victoria's default offer follows from October.
  • A skeptical minority argued the free hours are offset by higher prices the rest of the day, though others pushed back asking for evidence behind that claim.
10 Security

Cursor 0day: When Full Disclosure Becomes the Only Protection Left

359 points · 168 comments · mindgard.ai

Security firm Mindgard discloses a Cursor IDE flaw on Windows: when a project is opened, Cursor searches several locations for a git binary, including the workspace root itself, and will silently execute a malicious "git.exe" planted there — repeatedly, for as long as the project stays open — with no prompt, approval, or warning. Mindgard reported the bug on December 15, 2025, but despite months of follow-up, an admitted internal HackerOne workflow failure, and eventual engagement from Cursor's CISO, the issue remained unpatched more than six months and 197+ releases later, pushing the firm toward full public disclosure.

What the comments said

  • Many explained the likely mechanism: Windows' cmd.exe searches the current working directory for an executable before checking PATH, so a git.exe dropped in a repo root gets run instead of the real Git — a known Windows footgun that predates Cursor.
  • Commenters debated the attack surface, concluding that simply cloning and opening an untrusted repo (or checking out a malicious branch) is enough to trigger it, contrary to some who initially assumed a separate malicious download was required.
  • A number of commenters suspected the write-up itself was partly AI-generated, joking about timeline inconsistencies and suggesting that could partly explain the vendor's dismissive initial response.
  • Some pushed back on the proof-of-concept's framing, arguing Windows' SmartScreen/ACL should normally warn on new unsigned executables, so real-world exploitability depends on those protections being absent or bypassed.
  • Several expressed frustration that a company with 7M+ users and a reported $60B valuation went unresponsive for months over a straightforward, well-understood class of bug.
