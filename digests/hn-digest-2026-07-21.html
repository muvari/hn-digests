1 AI Economics

Who's afraid of Chinese models?

945 points · 830 comments · stratechery.com

Ben Thompson argues that Chinese open-weight models like Kimi K3 nearing the frontier are reviving marginal-cost economics in AI, upending the zero-marginal-cost dynamics that underpin his own Aggregation Theory. He distinguishes R&D (a fixed cost, so open weights aren't truly "free") from COGS/inference cost (which scales with revenue and is very real for AI serving). He then argues tokens are not a fungible commodity the way oil or copper are, since different models need different amounts of reasoning/agentic tokens to reach the same answer, so a cheaper-per-token model like Kimi can still lose on total cost if it needs far more tokens than OpenAI's Sol.

What the comments said

  • Commenters debate the fairness of frontier labs complaining about being distilled by Chinese competitors when those same labs built their models by scraping the open internet, with several arguing it's poetic justice
  • Sharp disagreement over whether the 'agent harness' is a real moat: one builder says model quality alone drives outcomes and harnesses barely matter, while others counter that harnesses matter mainly through vendor lock-in (subsidized plans tied to a provider's own app/UI)
  • Some commenters worry about geopolitical dependence on Chinese models and expect US restrictions similar to the TikTok divestiture or Huawei ban
  • Pushback on the article's 'tokens vs intelligence' framing: a commenter argues models that score identically on benchmarks aren't actually interchangeable in real-world use, predicting a future of specialized, mixed-model workflows rather than pure commoditization
  • General enthusiasm among commenters for the prospect of much cheaper inference driven by this competition

3 Mathematics

Human mathematicians are being outcounterexampled

465 points · 235 comments · xenaproject.wordpress.com

Mathematician Kevin Buzzard (of the Xena/Lean project) recounts a whirlwind few months in which AI systems disproved the Erdős Unit Distance conjecture, and that proof was then autoformalized into the Lean theorem prover, drawing on a deep 1960s Golod-Shafarevich number-theory result. He describes a competing effort where OpenAI's Sol model, steered by a human engineer, produced a full from-the-axioms Lean formalization consisting of 1.2 million lines of code in three weeks, more than the entirety of Lean's mathlib library took nine years to write. He also describes a workshop where an AI autoformalization tool caught a false claim in human-written mathematical exposition, finding yet another counterexample, and concludes that large AI-generated developments in mathematics are now inevitable.

What the comments said

  • Commenters note humans have been "outcounterexampled" by computer search for decades; what's new is that the same general-purpose system that finds counterexamples can also code, play chess, and write poetry, rather than being a narrow expert system built for one task
  • Debate over how much credit belongs to the AI versus the human mathematician who frames the problem and guides the search, since the search space is far too large for brute force and requires expert-crafted setup
  • A tangential but heavily discussed anecdote: Yitang Zhang's Jacobian conjecture thesis work was undermined decades ago by a flawed corollary from his advisor, costing him his academic career for years; commenters reflect on academic politics and speculate whether LLM-assisted checking could have prevented that
  • Philosophical split among commenters on whether disproof-by-counterexample is as valuable as a full proof: some say it efficiently redirects effort away from false statements, others argue proofs provide deeper understanding that mere counterexamples don't
  • Multiple commenters confirm and discuss that the post's author is well-known mathematician Kevin Buzzard, lending credibility to the account

Incremental – A library for incremental computations

325 points · 65 comments · github.com

This is the GitHub page for Jane Street's Incremental, an OCaml library for building self-adjusting computations, inspired by Umut Acar's research, that let complex calculations update efficiently when their inputs change rather than recomputing from scratch. Typical uses include spreadsheet-like calculations, GUI views that need to react to changing data, and derived data (like filtered or inverted mappings) that must stay in sync with a source.

96 points · 46 comments · compilade.net

The post works through a bit-packing scheme for ternary digits (trits), motivated by compressing BitNet b1.58's -1/0/1 model weights as close as possible to the theoretical log2(3) bits-per-trit limit. It shows that 5 trits pack into a single byte at 1.6 bits/trit (99% efficient), and the key trick is unpacking via fixed-point multiplication (multiply by 3, read the top bits) instead of slow modulo/division, making it SIMD-friendly for AVX2 and ARM NEON. The scheme is already merged into llama.cpp for BitNet and TriLM ternary models.

What the comments said

The unreasonable difficulty of time series forecasting

118 points · 43 comments · suzyahyah.github.io

The author benchmarks statistical models (Naive, AutoARIMA, Seasonal-Naive), neural models (DLinear, PatchTST), gradient-boosted trees, zero-shot foundation models (Chronos, TimesFM), and LLMs prompted on raw numeric sequences across datasets like M4, exchange rates, electricity, and bitcoin. It finds that for series with strong stable seasonality, simple statistical baselines and zero-shot foundation models beat sophisticated models by wide margins, while fancier models often do no better than a naive predictor and sometimes forecast in the wrong direction entirely. The post argues time series is fundamentally harder than iid machine learning or language modeling because observations come from a single correlated trajectory (a data-generating process, not distribution), giving low signal-to-noise, an effectively much smaller sample size than raw timestep counts suggest due to autocorrelation, and severe vulnerability to distribution shift.

Python 3.15’s Ultra-Low Overhead Interpreter Profiling Mode

143 points · 12 comments · fidget-spinner.github.io

CPython core developer Ken Jin describes a new low-overhead interpreter profiling technique built for Python 3.15's JIT trace recording. Rather than running two separate interpreters (too slow, roughly 6% slowdown) or gating profiling logic behind a runtime boolean flag (which still bloats and slows every opcode handler), the design swaps between two dispatch tables via a single variable: a profiling table routes every opcode through one shared recording instruction before falling back into the normal interpreter's dispatch. Benchmarks show this profiling scheme adds only about 4.5x overhead over the bare interpreter, dramatically less than meta-tracing systems like PyPy which can be 900-1000x slower.

What the comments said

  • Technically engaged commenters praise the dual dispatch-table trick specifically because it avoids bloating every opcode handler and avoids polluting the branch predictor history that keeps computed-goto dispatch fast
  • One commenter proposes an alternative implementation duplicating record-instruction labels before each opcode to save a jump, sparking a debate about whether that would bloat the instruction cache when profiling is off
