1 AI & Business

Who's afraid of Chinese models?

957 points · 862 comments · stratechery.com

Ben Thompson argues that open-weight Chinese models like Kimi K3 are reintroducing marginal costs into an AI industry that had assumed the zero-marginal-cost economics of software. He distinguishes R&D (a fixed, sunk cost) from COGS (inference cost, which scales with revenue), arguing open models aren't actually free to serve. He also pushes back on Nvidia's "token factory" framing, arguing tokens aren't a fungible commodity across models — different models need wildly different numbers of reasoning/agentic tokens to reach the same answer — so what's actually fungible is intelligence, not tokens, and true inference cost depends on model footprint, inference/memory/serving efficiency, and token efficiency together.

What the comments said

  • Commenters disputed the article's claim that agent harnesses are a moat: one described building a barebones harness and finding the model itself does nearly all the work, while others countered that harnesses matter as the "user touchpoint" and product surface (native apps, workflow integrations) that create real lock-in.
  • Several were simply happy about cheap, capable inference becoming available regardless of its geopolitical origin.
  • Others warned that reliance on Chinese models is itself a risk, predicting US policy moves against them similar to the TikTok divestiture or Huawei restrictions.
  • There was disagreement over whether models that produce the same benchmark answers are truly interchangeable, with some predicting increasing specialization and multi-model workflows rather than pure commoditization.

4 Mathematics

Human mathematicians are being outcounterexampled

483 points · 244 comments · xenaproject.wordpress.com

Kevin Buzzard (of the Xena/Lean formalization project) recounts a rapid string of events in which AI systems generated and then formally verified major counterexamples to open conjectures. ChatGPT disproved the Erdős Unit Distance conjecture, which was then autoformalized in the Lean proof assistant, first partially by Mike Freedman's Logical Intelligence and then completely "from the axioms" by OpenAI's Sol model, which generated 1.2 million lines of Lean code — more than the entire mathlib library took nine years of human effort to write. He also describes a formalization workshop where a rival tool found a counterexample disproving a claim in an LLM-generated exposition he'd prepared for his own Fermat's Last Theorem project, underscoring how fast AI-assisted counterexample-hunting and formal verification are now moving.

What the comments said

  • Some pushed back that computer-assisted counterexample search is decades old; what's new, per commenters, is a single system doing math, code, and other tasks generally rather than as a narrow expert system, plus far more compute.
  • Others noted humans still play an essential role framing problems and bounding the search space — the counterexample hunts weren't naive brute force but guided by expert prompting.
  • There was disagreement on the value of disproof by counterexample: some said it usefully saves wasted effort and sharpens conjectures, while others felt it's less illuminating than a constructive proof.
  • A widely discussed tangent recounted mathematician Yitang Zhang's career being derailed for years after a flawed corollary in his advisor's work invalidated part of his thesis, with commenters speculating how differently that might have played out with modern LLM and formal-verification tools available in 1986.
  • Several flagged the need for caution running AI-generated Lean code, since Lean is a full programming language and unsandboxed AI-written proofs could execute arbitrary commands.

6 Policy & Law

'VPNs are lawful technical tools,' says EU Court in landmark copyright ruling

559 points · 95 comments · techradar.com

The Court of Justice of the European Union ruled that VPNs are "lawful technical tools" and that a publisher using state-of-the-art geo-blocking is not liable for copyright infringement merely because a user circumvents it with a VPN. The case arose after Dutch and Belgian academic institutions published Anne Frank's manuscripts online, public domain in most countries but still copyrighted in the Netherlands until 2037; they geo-blocked Dutch visitors, and the Anne Frank Fonds sued, arguing that because VPNs make geo-blocking trivially bypassable, the site was effectively communicating the protected work to the Dutch public anyway. The court rejected that reasoning, holding that the mere possibility of VPN circumvention doesn't by itself make geo-blocking measures inadequate, while leaving it to a Dutch court to confirm whether the blocking was actually state-of-the-art.

What the comments said

  • Commenters clarified the ruling is narrowly about copyright liability, not a broad affirmation of VPN legality or privacy rights, cautioning against overreading it.
  • Several connected it to other recent EU/UK VPN battles — France ordering ISPs to block Polymarket without judicial review, and the UK scrapping plans to restrict VPNs for age-verification/online-safety enforcement — as part of a broader pattern of regulatory friction over circumvention tools.
  • Some found irony in geo-blocking content on the "World Wide Web," debating whether IP-based geographic restriction is a fundamental design flaw of the internet.
  • Others flagged that the ruling's requirement that geo-blocking be "state of the art and effective" leaves ambiguity that could still be used against VPN users in future cases.
  • A heated side-thread expressed cynicism about ongoing legislative efforts, particularly in France, to restrict VPNs and mandate age verification under the banner of child protection.

Apple defeats liability for not scanning iCloud for CSAM

415 points · 394 comments · blog.ericgoldman.org

A law-blog analysis of Amy v. Apple explains why a court dismissed claims that Apple should be liable for failing to scan iCloud for CSAM after abandoning its NeuralHash tool in favor of end-to-end encryption. The judge found Section 230 grants Apple full immunity because any duty to detect CSAM would require Apple to act as a publisher of third-party content, and none of the usual exceptions (Twitter's reporting-defect theory, Snap's own-content theory, Roommates.com's content-augmentation theory) apply. What the comments said

  • Others counter that Apple's own NeuralHash effort was a cautionary tale: it proved client-side scanning is technically feasible and "privacy preserving" in name, which worries commenters about the same tooling being repurposed to target dissidents.
  • The judge's framing of victimized children as "collateral damage" sparked debate, with some arguing children's safety is often used as a pretext to erode privacy and encryption more broadly.
  • A contentious subthread claimed most CSAM is self-generated and shared consensually among teens rather than produced by exploitative adults; other commenters pushed back and asked for citations.
  • Some replies mock-extend the judge's logic toward mandatory scanning of personal hard drives or physical homes, and note ambiguity about what actually counts as "end-to-end" encryption if the client app can still decrypt data.

15 Security

My USB Drive Has a Hidden Encrypted Vault

235 points · 139 comments · rootkitlabs.com

The author built Phantomdrive, a fully open-source USB drive that presents as an innocuous 8 GB drive until a password is entered into a specific plaintext file, at which point it unmounts and remounts a hidden AES-256-encrypted partition on an SD card, using a CH569 chip. The post walks through the key derivation function (a salted, 100,000-round SHA-256 chain chosen because the hardware can't afford a memory-hard KDF like Argon2 within a ~3-second unlock budget) and explains why naive AES-ECB is insecure compared to AES-CTR, while also noting the design responds to earlier AI-generated bug reports on the project's GitHub.

What the comments said

  • A prominent security-focused commenter argued off-the-shelf hidden-volume schemes won't fool state-level adversaries (vendors build detectors for known tricks) and that the KDF, constrained by the device's BOM, is far weaker than standard bcrypt and likely crackable in minutes against realistic passwords.
  • Others pushed back, arguing most real-world "state" threats are undertrained local officials or border agents unlikely to look for hidden volumes, with one anecdote about a drug raid that missed an in-plain-sight vaporizer.
  • Several noted a deeper deniability problem: publicly using a known "hidden drive" product undermines plausible deniability once an inspector recognizes the device.
  • Alternatives were floated, including VeraCrypt hidden volumes, dual-partition DOS+LUKS or OpenBSD setups, and filling free space on a FAT filesystem with information-theoretically indistinguishable encrypted data.
  • A few jokes about brute-forcing with rented GPU clusters, and one commenter's reminder: "you don't announce your security by obscurity methods."
