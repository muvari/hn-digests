Kimi K3: Open Frontier Intelligence

1,732 points · 1,015 comments · kimi.com

Moonshot AI announced Kimi K3, a 2.8-trillion-parameter open-weight model built on new "Kimi Delta Attention" and "Attention Residuals" architecture with a 1M-token context window and native vision, which it bills as the largest open model yet and roughly 2.5x more compute-efficient than its predecessor K2. 4 AI & Typography

Decoy Font

588 points · 138 comments · mixfont.com

Decoy Font is an experimental TTF typeface that uses "hybrid image" spatial-frequency tricks (the same technique behind the famous Einstein/Marilyn Monroe illusion) to encode two different messages in one piece of text: a foreground "decoy" message readable up close, and a real message visible only when blurred, squinted at, or viewed from a distance. The creator, building at Mixfont, demonstrates that this fools image-based AI readers like ChatGPT and Gemini into transcribing the decoy text, positioning it as a lightweight, downloadable alternative to their earlier motion-based "Ghost Font" for obscuring text from AI scrapers and OCR.

What the comments said

  • Several commenters questioned the practical use case, noting the technique also makes text hard for the human typing it to verify, and that it would badly break accessibility for screen reader users if ever used as a real UI font.
  • Multiple people tested it themselves and reported mixed results: some got both messages easily read by Claude or ChatGPT, while others (using newer prompts or models) got the decoy fooled as advertised, leading to skepticism about how cherry-picked the demo examples were.
  • One skeptical comment outlined a "methodology" critique: that the creator likely iterated the illusion against a specific model/prompt until it produced the desired decoy-fooling result before publishing, rather than the trick reliably working in general.
  • A few suggested improvements, like making both encoded letters "fake" so even browser-enabled AI agents copying raw text couldn't recover the real message, at the cost of usability.
  • Overall sentiment leaned toward finding it a fun, visually clever illusion/toy rather than a serious anti-AI security measure.

What the comments said

  • Some pointed out the practical wisdom of the move, noting bootstrapped founders regularly get blindsided by bad-faith trademark squatting, and that letting bad actors register first and then swooping in was the least-bad path politically.
