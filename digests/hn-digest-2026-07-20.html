2 Security

Hacker wipes Romania's land registry database

662 points · 370 comments · news.risky.biz

A hacker breached Romania’s national cadastre agency (ANCPI) and wiped the country’s entire land registry database after a failed extortion attempt, halting real-estate transactions nationwide for over a week. The intruder used valid credentials to map internal systems before destroying data and backups, and later put stolen employee credentials and internal documents up for sale on a hacking forum. Researchers identified the attacker as “ByteToBreach,” allegedly based in Algeria and previously linked to a breach of Sweden’s e-government portal. Romania joins Poland, Slovakia, Greece, Morocco, Russia, and Ukraine as countries whose land registries have been hacked in the past three years.

What the comments said

  • Commenters compared this to Slovakia’s nearly identical land-registry ransomware attack in January 2025, which similarly froze the real-estate market for about a month.
  • Many were relieved ANCPI appears to have had offline backups, avoiding the worst case of permanently unprovable land ownership.
  • A recurring theme, citing Romanian friends: several attributed the vulnerability to government IT contracts going to poorly-vetted cronies rather than a sophisticated attack.
  • Some questioned why “valid credentials” were used to gain entry, suspecting social engineering or insider corruption rather than a technical exploit.
  • A tangent half-joked about hoping the hack was a protest against private land ownership, prompting others to note Romania’s own communist history.

5 AI & Math

Claude Fable produced a counterexample to the Jacobian Conjecture

767 points · 479 comments · xcancel.com

A mathematician tweeted that Anthropic’s Fable model, working with him, produced an explicit polynomial map C³→C³ with constant nonzero Jacobian determinant that is nonetheless not injective — a counterexample disproving the long-standing Jacobian Conjecture. The claim was independently checked via Wolfram Alpha and by other AI models (Grok, GPT), which confirmed three distinct input points map to the same output. The conjecture, open since 1939, asked whether such maps must always have a polynomial inverse.

What the comments said

  • Commenters worked through the math themselves, clarifying the tweet’s confusing phrasing: three distinct inputs mapping to one output is exactly what breaks invertibility.
  • Many were struck by the informality of the announcement — a casual tweet thanking friends — for a result disproving an 87-year-old open conjecture.
  • Several linked related recent stories of LLMs contributing to other open math problems (the Cycle Double Cover conjecture, batches of Erdős problems), pointing to a broader pattern this year.
  • Others noted a formal Lean proof of the counterexample was already underway on GitHub within hours, addressing calls for rigorous verification beyond symbolic spot-checks.
6 Software Engineering

Perfection is not over-engineering

249 points · 109 comments · var0.xyz

The author argues the industry has wrongly equated “perfection” with “over-engineering”: over-engineering specifically means solving the wrong problem, usually from poorly gathered requirements, not excessive care. With a truly clear, tightly-constrained set of requirements, they argue, only one solution fits — and that solution is, by definition, the perfect one for that context. The piece reframes classic over-engineering (like three engineers running five microservices) as the product of solving problems a team never actually had, and argues the fix is treating systems as products and gathering requirements honestly.

What the comments said

  • Several disagreed with “over-engineering = wrong problem,” citing real examples of teams solving the right problem while still wildly overbuilding the solution.
  • Others rejected the idea of a single “perfect” solution at all, invoking Pareto optimality — most engineering involves genuine trade-offs, and over-investing in requirements gathering has its own cost.
  • A popular sub-thread objected to the “systems are products” framing as toxic, arguing the best software is built as a user-serving “tool,” not optimized against separate business goals.
  • Multiple commenters reframed “we’re not building the perfect solution” as usually meaning “we’re targeting the 90th percentile,” used to head off engineers who block shipping over rare edge cases.
  • Saint-Exupéry’s “perfection is achieved... when there is nothing left to take away” was cited approvingly by several as capturing their own philosophy.

9 Graphics & Game Dev

Corners Don't Look Like That: Regarding Screenspace Ambient Occlusion (2012)

176 points · 72 comments · nothings.org

A 2012 essay by Sean Barrett, resurfaced, arguing that screenspace ambient occlusion (SSAO) — used in games to darken corners and crevices — produces visually wrong results because real room corners usually aren’t actually dark; the effect is often confused with soft shadows and other lighting phenomena. The author backs this with his own photographs of real corners, analyzing pixel brightness across edges to show most perceived “AO darkening” in real scenes comes from directional shadows and Lambertian falloff rather than ambient occlusion itself.

What the comments said

  • Several agreed SSAO is a dated, often-abused hack, though newer techniques (ray-marched local radiosity, FidelityFX CACAO, full path tracing) are gradually improving on it.
  • A recurring counterpoint: SSAO was never meant to be physically accurate — it’s a cheap way to make geometry read clearly, not a lighting simulation, so critiquing its realism partly misses the point.
  • Some debated whether realism is even the right goal for games versus legibility and aesthetics, noting flat, information-poor lighting is often worse for gameplay than an imperfect AO hack.
  • A few pointed out most of the author’s example photos have obvious point-light sources and hard shadows, which they felt undermined blaming AO specifically.

What the comments said

  • Commenters were largely impressed by the methodology, especially the pre-ChatGPT control period establishing a false-positive floor, though some asked about possible training-data leakage between detector and evaluation set.

  • Some worried that as most CS papers turn AI-flavored, new researchers will unconsciously absorb “AI style” — though others argued sustained mimicry of AI phrasing across a whole document is actually hard to pull off.

You only need the frontier model for one single edit

171 points · 51 comments · stencil.so

A Stencil blog post argues the popular “expensive model plans, cheap model executes” agentic pattern is a false economy: since the vast majority of an agent’s token cost comes from reading and exploring code rather than editing it, handing a cheap model a plan document just makes it re-read everything the expensive model already read. Their proposed alternative, “/prewalk,” has the frontier model begin the task normally, make its first edit, then swaps to a cheap model with the planning instruction stripped from context — preserving actual explored context rather than a lossy summary, cutting cost by roughly half versus either model working alone.

What the comments said

  • Commenters broadly found the token-cost breakdown (9% edits vs. 91% reads) convincing and reported seeing the same pattern in their own agent usage.
  • Several described similar “smart model plans, cheap models execute, review the diff” workflows of their own, some noting an explicit review step catches mistakes the article’s comparison didn’t account for.
  • A side thread criticized the article’s own prose as clearly AI-generated and full of clichéd tech-blog phrasing, which some found ironic given the subject.
  • One commenter suggested simply instructing a router model to downgrade only when it estimates net cost savings, letting the AI make the model-selection call itself.

LoRA Speedrun is a “modded-nanogpt for fine-tuning”: a public, independently-verifiable wall-clock leaderboard for LoRA fine-tuning techniques, with two frozen tracks (GSM8K math on Qwen2.5-1.5B, SQuAD QA on SmolLM2-1.7B) run on identical hardware via free Modal compute credits, so competing speedup claims can be settled rather than remaining unfalsifiable marketing. The current GSM8K record, set within two days of launch, cut training time from an 11m57s baseline to 1m44s via aggressive data pruning and a custom packed training loop.

What the comments said

  • The most-discussed point was the maintainer’s admission that much of the project, including its README, was AI-generated, though the runs and numbers are real and independently reproducible — opinions split on whether that matters.
  • Several praised the frozen-task/frozen-hardware format itself, drawing a direct comparison to how the original nanoGPT speedrun produced the genuinely useful Muon optimizer.
  • Some worried a single task and model risks overfitting to that benchmark’s quirks; the maintainer agreed and said more tracks/models are planned specifically to test generalization.
  • A recurring, half-serious gripe: “LoRA” collides with the unrelated LoRa (Long Range) radio protocol acronym, causing regular confusion.

Sealed tomb filled with paintings and inscriptions discovered in Egypt

A Dutch archaeological mission from Leiden University, working in the Theban necropolis on Luxor’s West Bank, uncovered a sealed New Kingdom-era (likely Ramesside, 19th–20th Dynasty) tomb belonging to an official or priest named Paser, identified via inscriptions. The tomb follows the standard layout for Theban elite burials — an open courtyard, an inverted-T-shaped rock-cut chapel, and underground burial chambers — with painted scenes showing Paser and his wife in worship before deities and receiving offerings; cleaning and structural restoration are planned for future seasons.

When can a power company take your land for data center infrastructure?

169 points · 149 comments · theconversation.com

A legal scholar explains how power companies are increasingly using eminent domain — the government’s power to seize private property for “public use” with compensation — to acquire land for transmission lines needed to meet surging data-center electricity demand (data centers already used over 4% of US electricity in 2024). The piece traces the legal standard back to the Supreme Court’s 2005 Kelo v. City of New London decision and notes courts have reached mixed conclusions on whether lines serving primarily private data centers qualify as genuine “public use,” with outcomes varying significantly by state.

What the comments said

  • Commenters debated whether the article’s framing was misleading, since the eminent domain action targets land for grid transmission lines, not the data centers themselves directly.
  • Several were skeptical of the actual scale of announced data center demand, citing projects (a Utah proposal, a UK “Stargate” announcement) that turned out exaggerated, with one estimating only 30–40% of announced projects are financially real.
  • A detailed thread discussed why long-distance HVDC transmission lines (e.g. wind power from the central US to coastal cities) rarely get built, citing land acquisition and NIMBY opposition as the actual bottleneck rather than technology.
  • Multiple commenters argued the deeper issue is that data-center buildouts proceed without accounting for the transmission infrastructure they’ll require, effectively socializing the cost onto nearby communities and ratepayers after the fact.
