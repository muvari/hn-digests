Apple sues OpenAI, accuses ex-employees of stealing trade secrets

1,613 points · 922 comments · 9to5mac.com

2 Science

Einstein's relativity rules chemical bonds in heavy elements, new research shows

395 points · 182 comments · brown.edu

Brown University chemists published direct spectroscopic evidence in Science that the textbook picture of a triple bond — one sigma bond plus two pi bonds — breaks down in heavy elements. In carbon–bismuth molecules cooled to near absolute zero, photoelectron spectroscopy showed electrons moving fast enough that relativistic spin-orbit coupling smears the sigma/pi distinction, producing one pi bond and two sigma-pi hybrids instead. The idea that relativity matters in heavy-element chemistry dates to the 1970s, but this is claimed as the first direct experimental confirmation, with implications for bismuth's growing role in solar cells and quantum materials.

What the comments said

  • Much of the thread was collaborative science exposition: links to the Science paper and Dirac's equations, refreshers on sigma/pi bonds from AP chemistry, and the crowd-favorite fact that relativistic effects are why gold is gold-colored.
  • Several asked what's actually new here, since relativistic quantum chemistry has a whole Wikipedia page — the resolution being that this is the first direct experimental observation of the effect on bonding orbitals, not a new theory.
  • Pedants earned their keep: one commenter corrected the article's claim that nuclear mass causes the effect (it's the nuclear charge and the resulting Coulomb potential).
  • The mood was broadly delighted — one commenter mused, a couple of drinks in, about how experiments like this are what make science wonderful.

4 Databases

We scaled PgBouncer to 4x throughput

228 points · 53 comments · clickhouse.com

PgBouncer is single-threaded, so on a 16-vCPU box one core does all the connection pooling while fifteen idle. ClickHouse's Managed Postgres team runs a fleet of PgBouncer processes all binding the same port via so_reuseport, letting the kernel load-balance connections, and uses PgBouncer's peering feature to forward query-cancel requests to whichever process owns the session. On identical hardware the fleet hit ~336k transactions/sec versus ~87k for a single process — about 4x — while the single process left the machine 90% idle.

What the comments said

  • The top comment supplied the actual pgbouncer.ini config (so_reuseport, peer_id, [peers] section) that the article described but never showed.
  • Practitioners compared notes on alternatives: running multiple PgBouncers behind Kubernetes or HAProxy, with the reply that so_reuseport does the same job in kernel space without an extra userspace hop.
  • Others plugged competing poolers — Yandex's Odyssey and pgdog — and a ClickHouse engineer responded that they chose the most battle-tested option first, adding that they're not fans of 10k+ Postgres connections anyway ("100s are more than enough").
  • One commenter shared a delightfully gratuitous hobby version of the same trick: so_reuseport coordination with fd-passing between instances of a BitTorrent client.
5 Databases

Prefer strict tables in SQLite

318 points · 161 comments · evanhahn.com

Evan Hahn advocates for SQLite's underrated STRICT tables: append one keyword to CREATE TABLE and the engine rejects type mismatches (no more 'garbage' stored in an INTEGER column) and errors on bogus column types like DATETIME, JSON, or UUID that plain SQLite silently accepts. He covers the escape hatch (ANY columns), the caveats — you can't ALTER an existing table to strict, and it needs SQLite 3.37+ — and notes, fairly, that the SQLite developers themselves defend flexible typing as a feature.

What the comments said

  • The dominant sentiment by far: strict should be the default, and many were surprised it isn't. Simon Willison explained why it never will be — SQLite's near-absolute commitment to backwards compatibility means defaults essentially never change.
  • War stories backed up the advocacy, including a cleanup after the strings '1' and '0' were stored in a boolean column on thousands of deployed devices.
  • The gripes widened to SQLite's other legacy quirks: no real timestamp type (you get "yyyy-mm-dd HH:MM:SS" strings you must assume are UTC) and foreign-key enforcement being off by default.
  • Tone stayed affectionate — "a truly great project, with some truly baffling design decisions" captured the room.

9 Fintech

UPI: Anatomy of a Payment Transaction

215 points · 101 comments · timeseriesofindia.com

A richly illustrated walkthrough of what happens in the two seconds between scanning a QR code and the green tick in India's UPI system, which carried over 22 billion payments in June 2026 alone. The piece untangles the cast: apps like PhonePe and Google Pay (which hold no money and no banking license), the sponsor banks that lend them network access, and NPCI's central non-profit switch that routes every transaction, debiting the payer's bank before crediting the payee's.

What the comments said

  • Engineers reached for scale comparisons — someone who worked on scaling UPI explained why real-time inter-bank payments are far harder than exchange feeds: every transaction is a multi-party message dance completed while both users wait, 24/7, for free.
  • The thread turned into a system-design lesson: a national payment rail for 1.5 billion people averages "only" hundreds-to-thousands of TPS, a reality check for interview-style overengineering.
  • Comparisons with China's Alipay/WeChat Pay drew the sharpest technical distinction: those are closed custodial wallets, while UPI is a decentralized routing layer across real bank accounts, run at cost by a non-profit.
  • Genuine admiration dominated — UPI got even elderly users to go fully digital — tempered by a privacy minority uneasy about a centralized, KYC'd view of every transaction the state could switch off.
