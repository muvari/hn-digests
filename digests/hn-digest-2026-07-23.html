1 Psychology & Learning

Writing by hand is good for your brain

1,311 points · 589 comments · nealstephenson.substack.com

The essay (its host blocked direct fetching, so this summary is built from the piece's framing as quoted by commenters) argues handwriting is cognitively richer than typing: forming each letter means continuously solving small motor problems — spacing, joining strokes, crossing t's, dotting i's — using coordinated movement of the whole arm, not just the fingers. It leans on research showing that writing by hand, but not simply pressing keys, increases brain connectivity in the theta and alpha frequency bands, which the piece links to sensorimotor learning. The underlying argument is a broader case for preserving handwriting as typing, and now AI-generated text, become the default way people produce words.

What the comments said

  • Commenters challenged the causal leap: the cited studies show association between handwriting and increased brain connectivity, not proof that handwriting causes better learning — critics noted the effect could simply reflect that handwriting is a harder task, which recruits more of the brain regardless of benefit.
  • Several people described real habits that reinforce the piece's point, like using a reMarkable tablet or plain paper because handwritten notes stick better than typed ones, while conceding that searching and digitizing handwritten notes remains a real cost.
  • A vocal minority dismissed the “I'm not a neuroscientist, but...” framing as an appeal to authority not worth taking seriously, which others pushed back on by quoting the specific mechanistic claim being made.
  • The thread extended the theme to programming, with some arguing that coding without LLM assistance is “also good for your brain,” folding this into HN's recurring debate over whether AI tools erode skill.
  • The story was flagged early as a dupe of a previous submission covering the same essay.

  • One reply expanded on the article's verification framing with a shared taxonomy of constraint mechanisms for steering agents — generative (constraining model output), interpretive (constraining how the model understands code), and elicitative (helping it ask the right questions) — linking to a longer external write-up on the idea.
