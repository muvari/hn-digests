GigaToken: ~1000x faster Language model tokenization

591 points · 117 comments · github.com

Gigatoken is a new tokenizer claiming up to ~1000x the throughput of HuggingFace's Rust-based tokenizers library, positioned as a near drop-in replacement compatible with both HuggingFace and tiktoken tokenizer APIs. It's pip-installable, hits gigabyte-per-second throughput across common x86 and ARM CPUs and across a wide range of existing tokenizer vocabularies, though its "compatibility mode" (matching HF output exactly) is notably slower than its native API.

What the comments said

  • The author explained in-thread that the gains come from replacing regex-based pretokenization with heavily SIMD-optimized code, minimizing branching, aggressively caching pretoken-to-token mappings despite long-tailed distributions, and minimizing Python/native interop overhead.
  • Some questioned the real-world value since tokenization is typically under 0.1% of total inference time; others countered that at industry-wide inference scale (citing a ~$28B/year estimate) even a small slice is a meaningful workload, and that fast tokenization matters for token-budget checks, routing/rate-limiting, and reducing time-to-first-token on cached prefixes.
  • The author noted the primary motivating use case is pretraining pipelines, where tokenizing massive datasets (e.g., DCLM) across large CPU fleets for days at a time is a genuine bottleneck.
  • General admiration for the engineering effort itself, with some commenters arguing that squeezing out extreme performance is worthwhile as an end in itself regardless of practical necessity.
6 Programming

Everyone should know SIMD

590 points · 221 comments · mitchellh.com

Mitchell Hashimoto argues SIMD's reputation as an obscure, expert-only optimization is undeserved: most useful SIMD code follows one repeatable five-step shape — broadcast constants, loop one vector-width chunk at a time, perform the vectorized operation across lanes, reduce/store the result, and handle the leftover scalar tail. Using Zig in his examples (though the concepts generalize to any language), he argues everyday engineers, not just authors of libraries like simdjson, should know this pattern, and explains why compilers generally can't auto-vectorize this reliably on their own.

What the comments said

  • Debate over auto-vectorization: some suggested just compiling with -O3, but others — including compiler engineers — countered that reliable auto-vectorization is rare in practice and usually requires restructuring data from array-of-structs to struct-of-arrays, a change compilers can't make unilaterally.
  • A detailed exchange about Zig's SIMD support, with Hashimoto replying directly in-thread: some "vector" builtins secretly unpack and loop per element instead of operating on the whole vector, much of std.math is scalar-only, and Zig's vectors are compile-time-sized only — which is why his Ghostty project pulls in Google's Highway C++ library for runtime CPU-feature dispatch instead of native Zig vectors.
  • Several stressed that data layout and access patterns (data-oriented design) matter more than SIMD itself; without contiguous, cache-friendly layouts, vectorization gains are largely wasted — one compared it to "putting racing tires on a lemon with a broken engine."
  • A Casey Muratori talk about optimizing for the game The Witness was cited repeatedly as the canonical demonstration of carrying SIMD gains all the way from problem definition down to instructions.
  • Some preferred explicit, predictable vectorization over relying on compiler or JIT behavior, noting that unrelated code changes or compiler updates can silently regress a hot loop back to scalar execution.

Are AI labs pelicanmaxxing?

647 points · 237 comments · dylancastillo.co

  • A tangent on specialized SVG-generation models (e.g. Recraft V4) producing noticeably sharper pelican images than general-purpose LLMs.
  • A few push back on the scoring methodology, suggesting pairwise ELO comparisons would be more rigorous than the 1-5 judge ratings, though others say that level of rigor is overkill for a joke benchmark.
  • Discussion of what "benchmaxxing" even means here, with some noting that getting better at SVG generation is itself a genuinely useful skill, not just gaming a metric.

15 UX Design

Ghost Cut – Or why Cut and Paste is broken everywhere

183 points · 133 comments · ishmael.textualize.io

The author argues cut & paste has three flaws baked into virtually every editor: a cut can't be truly undone because clipboard changes survive an undo; cutting instantly reflows the document, losing your place; and cut+paste isn't atomic, requiring multiple undo steps to fully reverse. Their fix, "Ghost Cut" (built into their Ishmael editor), makes ctrl+x fade the selected text into an inert, still-present state without touching the clipboard at all; escape restores it, while pasting elsewhere atomically removes it from the original spot and inserts it at the cursor, fully undoable in a single stroke. They'd like other editors to adopt it, though they concede the case is weaker for code than prose.

What the comments said

  • Multiple commenters note Excel already does something similar (fading cut cells) and say it's their least favorite cut/paste experience, citing broken formula references.
  • Several suggested drag-and-drop already solves the "move text" case the author describes, though others countered that's mouse-dependent and awkward for keyboard-first users.
  • Some drew a parallel to Windows Explorer's file-cut behavior (files gray out until pasted), noting it still uses the clipboard unlike Ghost Cut.
  • A common pushback: framing today's behavior as a "flaw" is disputed — several argue leaving cut text on the clipboard after an accidental cut is a deliberate, sensible safety net, not a bug.
  • A few commenters said they actively rely on the current "broken" behavior — cutting, undoing the document edit, but keeping the clipboard contents to paste elsewhere — and worry Ghost Cut would break that workflow.
